Pure Rogue Apr 23, 2026 #

v0.4 — Hut Interiors, Dungeons, Full Audio, and a Major AI Overhaul

This is the biggest update Pure Rogue has had.

Huts now have real interiors. Walk through the door and an entirely separate room renders in an overlay — with its own physics, combat, enemies, chests, and objects. Everything inside is independent from the exterior. Exit by walking south past the boundary. It sounds like a small detail and it makes the world feel much more like a world.

Underground rooms are actual dungeons now. Navigate between floors using v and ^. Each floor has its own generated layout and enemy set. Minable rocks now reliably drop stone, which was a long-standing complaint from the one playtester who is me.

Enemies have movement personalities. This is the one I'm most proud of. Five archetypes:

  • Chaser — direct pursuit, no hesitation
  • Keeper — holds a preferred range, punishes players who rush in
  • Kiter — circle-strafes and times its attack rushes
  • Jumper — discrete zigzag lunges, erratic and hard to predict
  • Ambusher — stationary until you get too close, then an explosive burst

Each zone has a personality profile that draws from the archetypes that fit its character. Fighting in Cyan feels different from fighting in Red — not just because the enemies look different, but because they move differently. That's the thing I was aiming for.

Zone-aware spawning is in. Green, Red, Cyan, Yellow, and Gray each have tiered enemy tables that scale with depth. No more generic bleed between zones.

Audio is fully layered now. Dynamic music with layers that respond to game state, plus SFX for aggro, blade attacks, bow charge, dodge roll, item destruction, and player death.

Lots of bug fixes too. Play it here.

Pure Rogue Mar 22, 2026 #

v0.3, a First Boss, and Two Instruments

March was a big month for Pure Rogue.

v0.3 shipped with dynamic music, unlockable characters (find captive heroes to extend and enhance your run), themed zones with distinct enemy rosters, discoverable secrets, a reworked crafting menu, and a balance pass across items and probabilities. The kind of update that changes what the game feels like to play, not just what's in it.

Then I coded my first boss. It felt great. Some things are immediately fun to build, and that was one of them — I'll leave the details for when you find it.

The sound design is worth talking about separately. I gave myself a hard constraint going in: two instruments only. Square wave and noise.

Square wave has no dynamics — no louder, no softer — so all the expressiveness has to come from duration and pitch. Short notes read as quieter. Low tones recede into the background. The only way to add complexity is to layer, so the green zone battle music is three discordant low square waves stacked on each other. It makes a guttural, churning sound that somehow never overstays its welcome.

Noise is technically cheating. Noise can do almost anything. But it's what makes weapon SFX feel physical and percussive instead of just tonal, so I'm allowing it and pretending I'm not.

Each colored zone is supposed to feel musically distinct. I have green down. The others are still developing. I'm waiting for the right ideas rather than filling the space with obvious ones. The constraint is useful that way too: it slows you down enough to be patient.

Devlog Feb 24, 2026 #

Got Distracted by DiceWars. Also Shipped Pure Rogue.

I've been playing DiceWars since at least 2008. It's a browser game — territory control, dice mechanics, Risk without the three-hour commitment. Simple, fast, genuinely tactical. It's also open source, and there are a handful of things about it that have always bothered me: the animations are slow, the audio is intrusive, the pacing drags in ways that have nothing to do with the strategy.

In February I finally fixed it. Stripped the animations, reworked the pacing, removed the sound effects, and shipped a version I'd actually want to play. It lives at ceruleanoak.github.io/dicewars/reloaded and it took about a week. Sometimes the distractions are the work.

Then — because apparently February had energy to spare — I formally published Pure Rogue v0.2. The ASCII roguelike I'd been building in parallel, the one I posted a demo of in a YouTube short. It's real now. You can play it, die in it, and wonder why you went north when you clearly should have gone west.

v0.2 is a foundation: real-time combat, procedural rooms, crafting with hidden recipes, multiple enemy types, the dual REST/EXPLORE loop. Not everything I want it to be, but everything it needs to be to be a game. The plan from here is content and feel — more enemies, more weapons, more reasons to push deeper than you probably should.

Penumbra Jan 18, 2026 #

Closing In on Full Migration

January was heads-down work that doesn't make for exciting updates. The kind of progress that matters but doesn't photograph well.

Collisions are finalized. Camera is dialed in. Saving and loading are confirmed working with a player-facing menu — replacing the old designer-only version that had no business being seen by anyone. The foundation is stable.

What's left is platforms (with real physics) and enemies. Enemies are the game's entire variety beyond room layouts. They're what makes each run feel different, what forces improvisation, what creates the moments players remember and describe to other people. So this isn't the boring part left over. It's the most interesting part, deferred until the architecture was solid enough to hold it.

I've also been studying. C++ has a learning curve that GameMaker's scripting language doesn't, and I'd rather understand the language I'm working in than reverse-engineer my own code six months from now. Slows things down short-term. Worth it.

Getting close to something I can actually call a game again.

Penumbra Dec 30, 2025 #

The C++ Engine Runs Crisp (The Player Is Technically Present)

The bugs that used to haunt me in GameMaker are gone.

I want to say that again because it felt genuinely surprising: the engine is running, the collisions work, and the weird persistent glitches I'd been working around for months have just stopped. Because I own every layer now. There's no platform making decisions I can't see or override.

The player is technically invisible — no sprite work done yet, just a hitbox moving around a tile grid — but the behavior is correct and the framerate is what I wanted. Nearest-neighbor scaling is fixed, which sounds minor and is everything for pixel art. The old version was blurring the low-res textures. Now they're sharp. The render order is properly 3D.

I spent a while just moving a ghost player through rooms watching the collision detection behave exactly as intended. This is hard to explain to someone who hasn't done it: the specific satisfaction of invisible infrastructure working correctly. No visual feedback, just numbers doing what they're supposed to do. It was a hallelujah moment.

End-of-month status: player object, controls, grid rendering, room layouts, textures, debug layer, render layers, camera (mostly), collision detection, saving and loading all done. Platforms and enemies are what's left. Those are the hard parts. They're also the fun parts.

Penumbra Oct 1, 2025 #

The Jury Is In on GameMaker

I've been in deliberations with myself about GameMaker for a while. October is when the verdict came in.

GameMaker is a good tool. It's a particularly good tool if your game fits neatly inside the assumptions it was built around — tile-based 2D, sprite-driven, event-based logic, GML as your scripting layer. For a lot of games, that's everything you need and the tradeoffs are worthwhile.

Penumbra is an isometric game with a custom camera system, a rendering pipeline I need full control over, and systems that have grown to the point where the platform is doing less and less of the work and resisting me more and more. My camera system hit 3,000 lines. That's not a GameMaker problem, exactly — that's a me problem — but GameMaker made it harder to fix than it needed to be.

The transition options are JavaScript, Python, or C++. JavaScript is where I'm most fluent. Python has the ergonomics but not the performance profile I want for a real-time game. C++ is the hard choice and the right one for what Penumbra actually needs: direct memory control, genuine performance headroom, and the GLSL shader pipeline I've been wanting to use properly.

I don't regret the time in GameMaker. The prototype exists because of it. But this is where the project outgrows the training wheels, and they have to come off.

Devlog Sep 2, 2025 #

I Built My Own WaniKani Instead of Working on the Game

WaniKani, if you're not familiar, is a web app for learning Japanese kanji through spaced repetition. It's well-designed, it works, and it's a subscription cloud service — which means your progress lives on someone else's server, billed monthly, inaccessible if the service goes down or goes away.

That bothers me more than it probably should. So in September, instead of working on the game, I built a replacement.

The system uses SMTP to send scheduled emails — review sessions, quiz cards, progress reports — all generated and delivered by my home server on a schedule I control. The review intervals are adaptive. The progress data is mine. There's no dashboard, no mobile app, no account recovery flow, because I don't need any of that. I need flashcards in my inbox at 7am.

This is stage two of what I'm calling the off-grid plan: moving my personal infrastructure — goals, habits, language study, exercise and diet tracking — away from cloud services entirely. It sounds like a lot of work for something I could just pay $10/month for. It is a lot of work. I find it satisfying in the same way I find game development satisfying: building something that does exactly what you need and nothing else.

The game prototype is still going, for the record. The core system is solid. I just want to give it another month of content before I let anyone else near it.

Devlog Aug 3, 2025 #

Fifteen Years of Not Being a Programmer, Then This

I want to take a moment to acknowledge something, because it keeps catching me off guard.

Two years ago, I was not a programmer. I'd been designing games my entire life — sketching mechanics, building paper prototypes, obsessing over systems — but I always relied on other people's tools. GameMaker. Scratch. Unity before I bounced off it. I never actually learned to code. I told myself I wasn't a programmer person. I pussyfooted around it for fifteen years.

Now I'm building repos, publishing games, configuring MCP servers and APIs, managing a project backlog in Airtable, running a home server that emails me Japanese flashcards on a schedule. I moved my entire Penumbra workflow out of the GameMaker IDE and into VS Code this week, with GitHub wired up for permanent version control. None of this is exotic — working developers do all of it — but two years ago it was completely outside my world.

I want to be direct about what made the difference: generative AI. Not as a crutch, not as a shortcut, but as the thing that finally made the gap between "I know what I want to build" and "I know how to build it" crossable. I've made three game prototypes, two unfinished jam projects, and a pile of experiments. I've become, in a real and functional sense, a programmer.

12-year-old me wanted to make his own Chrono Trigger. I have a full-time job and raise three kids. It's crazy. And it's happening.

Also: the camera system is now 3,000 lines long and I just broke it into pieces. The code is more manageable. Whether it was worth the refactor effort is genuinely unclear. We have a home server now after 15 years. Progress continues on all fronts.

Penumbra Jul 27, 2025 #

Enemies Exist. Also: Clown Fiesta.

The enemy system has been railed and derailed a few times. I'd rather show enemies off when they're both pretty and fully fledged, so I've been holding back. But I can at least report on what exists: enemies are in. They can push each other around. They can damage the player. They can bounce in an arc. They can travel in paths or in a set direction. The building blocks are there.

This week I ran a stress test — more objects in a single room than I would ever actually put in the game — and the results were what I'd call a clown fiesta. Platforms bouncing everything everywhere, coins getting pushed across the floor by moving geometry, enemies getting stuck in unlikely pile-ups. Performance was completely fine, which is the important part. Collisions between the player, enemies, platforms, and coins are all working. The render order, however, is visually out of control.

The render order problem is an inherent challenge in isometric games and I've been putting it off. Isometric is 2D — there's no real depth, no draw distance, no 3D vector positions. Everything you see is an illusion maintained by careful layering. When you have moving objects at different heights all overlapping each other, deciding which one draws on top requires constant recalculation. I have an idea for an elegant solution, and it is definitely crazy, which means I have to try it.

Someone in the server compared it to FEZ, which I'll take. There's a whole lost generation of isometric games I wasn't even aware of before starting this project. The perspective is genuinely interesting — it lends itself to claustrophobia and a kind of spatial ambiguity that flat 2D doesn't have. I'm starting to understand why people built entire careers around it.

Penumbra May 25, 2025 #

Why Render Order Is the Hardest Problem in an Isometric Game

This month had a milestone I want to actually explain, because "fixed the rendering pipeline" doesn't convey why it matters.

Isometric is 2D. The sense of depth — objects in front of and behind each other, height differences, layered floors — is entirely an illusion maintained by the order in which things are drawn. There is no 3D engine handling it. Every frame, the game has to decide: when this platform moves, when the player walks, when an object changes position, what order do I draw everything so the result looks spatially correct?

I completely overhauled how the game handles this. Now, anytime an object moves, the engine recalculates draw order for everything affected. The player always renders on top of the floor. Platforms that are elevated render above platforms that aren't. It handles edge cases that previously looked wrong. It's not a flashy change — you'd only notice if it were broken — but it's one of the foundational pieces the rest of the game is built on.

Also new this month: a coin spill mechanic. Take damage or fall too far and your coins scatter — retrievable, but at the cost of a moment of vulnerability. It's the kind of mechanic that sounds simple and does a lot of work: it makes damage feel consequential without ending your run, and it creates those scramble moments where you have to decide whether the coins are worth the risk of staying exposed.

Sprites are in progress. Walls are in. The prototype is starting to look like something a human could actually play.

Penumbra Apr 13, 2025 #

Now I Have to Learn Aseprite

A few things came together this month that make the prototype feel significantly more like a game.

Tile type selection is now in the editor menu, which makes building rooms much faster. More importantly: landing and crashing is in. Fall too far and you'll pay for it — the mechanic punishes careless navigation without being punishing in a way that stops being fun. Getting the feel right took some tuning, but it's there.

Half-height tile differences are also supported now. This sounds like a small thing and it does a lot: varying the height of surfaces increases visual interest and gives the player more navigation options without adding complexity to the systems underneath. A room with only flat floors reads as flat. A room with a few half-steps becomes a space you actually want to explore.

Room transitions are cleaned up and looking good. The moment of moving between rooms is now smooth enough that I don't need to think about it — which is the bar for that kind of thing.

Next up: sprites. The placeholder art has been getting the job done, but it's time to actually learn Aseprite and stop being jank about it.

Penumbra Nov 30, 2023 #

The Part Where I Decided to Make My Own Chrono Trigger

There's a thing that happens when you're an adult with responsibilities and you sit down to make the game you always wanted to make as a kid. You realize immediately that twelve-year-old you had no idea how long this would take. You do it anyway.

That's where this started. November 2023, GameMaker, a blank project, and an idea for a game set in a citadel full of puzzle rooms run by robots and a master AI. You rescue characters who become playable — each with different abilities — and losing them sends you back to clear rooms you'd already bypassed. A game that plays differently every time, rewards both caution and recklessness, and asks the player to decide whether they're ready for a room before they enter it.

The first month got further than I expected. Collision detection working. Room switching. Enemy detection and stealth. Fake 3D elevation — a layer-switching system so certain characters can access underground paths. Character swapping with different stats. The "digger" character concept: access underground tunnels in dirt areas, hide from enemies, slip under fences. Areas of a room that only specific characters can reach. The push and pull of choosing whether to risk losing a character on a room you might not be equipped for.

The game is called Penumbra. It's being built in the margins of a full-time job and raising three kids — which makes some people say "I don't know how you find the time" and others say "you probably shouldn't." Both are correct.

Everything on this site came out of that first month. The roguelike, the card game, the strategy mod — all of it is a side effect of sitting down and refusing to not make something.
